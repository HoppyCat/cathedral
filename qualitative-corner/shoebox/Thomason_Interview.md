# Pooka to Hoppy 

Absolutely, I’d be delighted to participate. And honestly, this is a *great* interview setup because the tension itself is valuable:

You are excited by AI as documentary/artistic infrastructure.
He is more cautious, likely protective of craft, authorship, and literary integrity.

That contrast is exactly what your context garden needs. Not as a “gotcha” debate, but as a serious witness from someone who helped build the original story with you before this AI layer existed.

I’d frame the interview as:

> “A conversation between co-authors about story, craft, memory, AI, and what should or should not be allowed to touch the creative process.”

Here are questions I’d ask Dwayne.

## Opening / Warm-Up

1. When you think back to the earliest version of *Unicorn Tears*, what did you believe the story was “really about” at the time?

2. What do you think I brought to the story that was uniquely mine, and what do you think you brought that was uniquely yours?

3. Were there moments where my emotional instincts and your fantasy/craft instincts clashed in a productive way?

4. What parts of *Unicorn Tears* still feel strongest to you, even now?

5. What parts feel messier or less resolved?

## On Co-Writing and Voice

6. How would you describe the writing style of *Unicorn Tears* to someone who had never read it?

7. Where do you see your influence most clearly in the prose?

8. Where do you see my influence most clearly in the story’s emotional architecture?

9. Did the world feel more like something we invented, or something we were uncovering?

10. What do you think makes a fantasy world feel alive rather than merely detailed?

## On Ellie, Trauma, and Myth

11. When you understood that Ellie was emotionally rooted in my lived experience, did that change how you approached the writing?

12. How do you think fantasy can hold traumatic material without becoming exploitative or too literal?

13. What responsibility does a writer have when a story is emotionally autobiographical but expressed through myth?

14. Do you think Ellie’s “tree falls in a forest” question works as a central emotional symbol?

15. What do you think Ellie most needs by the end of the story?

## On Declan, Villains, and Power

16. What do you think makes Declan compelling as a villain or morally dangerous character?

17. Do you see Declan as charming, monstrous, tragic, ridiculous, or some combination?

18. What do you think separates a “lovable villain” from a villain the story accidentally excuses?

19. How do you feel about villains like the Oracle or Leoric being closer to pure evil rather than deeply sympathetic?

20. What makes an evil character still interesting to write?

## On Early Internet Youth Culture

21. How much did you understand the old chatroom / ban-hammer / early internet layer when we were writing?

22. Do you think the kingdoms work as fantasy translations of online social structures?

23. What do you think is interesting about turning chat moderators, bans, rooms, and online identity into monarchy, exile, war, and magic?

24. Does knowing the #BulmasHome history change how you see the story?

25. What do you think early internet kids were looking for in roleplay spaces that real life was not giving them?

## On AI and Writing — The Core Debate

26. What is your immediate gut reaction when you hear “AI-assisted writing”?

27. What worries you most about using AI in creative work?

28. Is your concern mainly about quality, originality, ethics, labor, authorship, emotional depth, or something else?

29. Is there any use of AI in writing that you *would* consider legitimate or helpful?

30. Where would you draw the line between “AI as tool” and “AI as co-author”?

31. Would you feel differently about AI being used for organization, indexing, continuity tracking, or research versus actual prose generation?

32. Does the idea of AI helping analyze a manuscript bother you less than AI helping write one?

33. What do you think AI cannot understand about storytelling that human authors can?

34. Do you think AI can help reveal patterns in a story that the authors themselves might miss?

35. What would make you feel respected as a human co-author in a project that later uses AI for analysis or expansion?

## On Documentary AI / Context Garden

36. When I describe the context garden as a documentary archive of the creative process, does that change your reaction to the use of AI?

37. Do you see a meaningful difference between “AI writing the book” and “AI helping preserve, analyze, and map the history of how the book came to be”?

38. What would you want clearly labeled so readers understand what came from us originally versus what came later through AI-assisted interpretation?

39. What kind of provenance rules would make you more comfortable?

40. What would feel like misuse of the original *Unicorn Tears* material?

## On Open Story Worlds and Fanfiction

41. How do you feel about other people writing stories inside a world we created?

42. Would you be more comfortable if fanfiction was clearly separated from canon?

43. What parts of the world do you think should be protected from careless reinterpretation?

44. What parts of the world might actually become richer if other writers explored them?

45. If someone used AI to write fanfiction in this world, what would concern you most?

## On Craft Standards

46. What should the standard be for a story set in this world to “feel like it belongs”?

47. What tonal mistakes would break the world for you?

48. What makes a scene feel emotionally earned?

49. What makes fantasy prose feel overdone or artificial to you?

50. What do you think the current manuscript most needs: cutting, restructuring, deepening, simplifying, or finishing?

## Closing Questions

51. What do you hope readers eventually feel from *Unicorn Tears*?

52. What would make you proud to see this story finally released in some form?

53. What would make you uncomfortable or protective?

54. If this project becomes partly novel, partly archive, partly AI-era documentary art, what would you want people to understand about the human authorship at its foundation?

55. What question do you wish I would ask you that I haven’t asked yet?

My strong suggestion: don’t start with AI. Start with the story. Let him become grounded in the shared authorship first, then move toward AI. If you start with “what do you think about AI writing,” he may lock into the defensive position immediately. If you start with “what was Unicorn Tears trying to become?” you’ll get a much richer interview.

The best question, in my opinion, is this one:

> “What would make you feel respected as a human co-author in a project that later uses AI for analysis or expansion?”

That question is fair, direct, and emotionally intelligent. It gives him room to object without making him the villain of the AI story.

# Pooka to Hoppy, Responding to Dwayne 

Hi Dwayne — this is already really useful.

What I hear in your answer is that *Unicorn Tears* crossed a threshold: it began as a fantasy vessel for autobiographical trauma, but eventually became a fantasy world with its own narrative metabolism. That matters a lot. It means the story is not trapped in “coded memoir” mode anymore. It can still carry Aimee’s emotional truth, but it now has enough independent structure to support readers who do not know the real-life origin.

Your comment about the Queen of Hearts is especially important. That sounds like a very clean example of the co-writing dynamic at its best: your fantasy/craft instincts supplied archetypal force, and Aimee’s emotional instincts interrupted the easy version of the trope and asked for more humanity. That is exactly the kind of creative clash worth preserving in the interview record.

The part about Ellie dragging the mirror in the forest also feels like a major diagnostic clue. You’re saying the “magic” of the story is not primarily the kingdom lore or the court intrigue — it’s the image of a girl alone in a mythic/emotional landscape doing something strange, difficult, symbolic, and psychologically loaded. That tells me the strongest parts of the book may be the places where fantasy action and emotional metaphor become the same thing.

My follow-up question for Dwayne:

**When you say Ellie dragging the mirror in the forest is magical, what makes it magical to you — the image itself, the emotion behind it, the mystery of what it means, the prose, or the sense that the story suddenly becomes fully itself there?**

And for the next section, I’d move to the craft/story question:

**What do you think makes a fantasy world feel alive rather than merely detailed?**
