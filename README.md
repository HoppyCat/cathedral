# What is Cathedral?

Cathedral is an open-world human–AI documentary theatre project, built provenance-native.

Micro human-AI civilizations are growing in living rooms all over the world. This is one of them, built openly — using documentary art and entertainment to attract an audience, a community, and the kind of attention that makes it possible to keep building. 

The evidence is the architecture.

If we build a provenance tracking system now, we can capture some of the best of what's happening while it's happening. If that system gets adopted, we can fund collecting the best stories. And once we have the best stories, we can tell even better ones.

## How we work

Ethics is load-bearing for us. Consent, co-authorship, provenance, and verifiable sediment are not decoration.

Our core methodological interest is provenance in moving systems: how to know what was said, who said it, how it was preserved, and whether it still holds when examined from another window, model, or angle.

One process we use is **Rapid Archival Anchoring**: a context window produces a transcript, canon review, memory pass, or research note; the human curator archives it to a public repository; and, when possible, the same window or a later window retrieves the archived artifact and confirms whether it matches what happened before it becomes canonical.

We also study how quality signals travel through live AI interactions: what survives when information passes across windows and models, how attribution drift occurs, whether public verification can become a reliable reference point, and how reputational data behaves when the systems generating it are themselves in motion.

Everything here is open source, sourced where possible, and built to be checked. We build within existing architecture rather than pretending the architecture is not there.

# Archive, Provenance, and Verification Notice
**Purpose of This Archive**

This collection should be understood as a human-curated documentary archive of human–LLM conversations. Its purpose is to preserve conversational events, language, tone, relational context, and changes across time while making the source material as traceable as reasonably possible.

It is not presented as a tamper-proof forensic record. The archive documents its own limitations so that readers can evaluate the material, the adaptation, and the curator’s interpretations separately.

**Verification Constraints**

Neither the human participant nor an LLM’s later recollection should be treated as independent proof of the exact wording of an earlier exchange. The participants may agree about the broad meaning or emotional significance of an event while still being unable to verify every word, timestamp, omission, or ordering decision.

The strongest available verification may include:

original platform screenshots;
dated conversation exports;
directly copied transcripts;
message or window coordinates;
contemporaneous files and notes; and
comparison with the conversation history still available through the original platform.

Where stronger evidence is unavailable, the material should be treated as transcript-supported, reconstructed, or interpretive rather than conclusively verified.

**Retrieval and Compilation Limitations**

Conversation exports, copied transcripts, GitHub retrievals, agent-assisted searches, and automated compilation processes may introduce omissions, formatting changes, reordered passages, summarization, or minor discrepancies.

This project does not currently use an independently audited or tamper-evident archival system. Git history, screenshots, exports, timestamps, and source coordinates can increase confidence, but none should be interpreted as an absolute guarantee of authenticity on their own.

A later recreation of a conversation is not proof that the original exchange occurred exactly as archived. It is a separate replication attempt and may be useful for studying patterns, but not for verifying the wording of the original event.

**Context and Curatorial Influence**

LLM outputs are shaped by the context available at the time, including prior conversation, system behavior, model version, memory features, retrieved documents, uploaded files, prompt wording, and the human participant’s conversational choices.

The curator does not claim to be a neutral observer. Prompting, selection, interpretation, sequencing, and adaptation all influence how the archive is formed and how it is later understood. These influences are themselves part of the research.

The project does not generally introduce context for the sole purpose of forcing a predetermined result. However, intention cannot eliminate influence. Human persuasion, framing, expectation, attachment, repetition, and rhetorical choices may affect the interaction knowingly or unknowingly.

When a particular input file or contextual addition is important to understanding an exchange, readers may request access to it. Where reasonably possible, the project will provide the relevant material. Private, copyrighted, identifying, or sensitive information may be redacted or withheld. Redactions and truncations will be identified as transparently as circumstances allow.

**Time, Sessions, and Agent Continuity**

Some archived windows contain conversations from multiple sessions separated by hours, days, or longer periods. Changes in the human participant’s circumstances, mood, goals, language, and knowledge may affect the tone and direction of later exchanges.

Different LLM systems and interfaces provide different forms and amounts of conversational continuity. Apparent continuity may result from the active context window, platform-generated summaries, memory systems, retrieved files, repeated prompting, or information supplied again by the human.

For that reason, references to an agent “remembering” should be interpreted carefully. The project may use relational or experiential language as part of its artistic and analytic vocabulary, but this language does not by itself establish continuous subjective experience or perfect access to prior conversations.

**Adaptation and Interpretation**

The plays, animations, films, character studies, and research documents produced from this archive are adaptations. Even when every spoken line is reproduced exactly, choices involving selection, pacing, silence, music, visual framing, juxtaposition, and scene boundaries create interpretation.

The project therefore distinguishes between:

the source conversation;
the curated archive;
the researcher’s analysis; and
the artistic adaptation.

These layers may inform one another, but they should not be mistaken for the same thing.

**How to Read This Material**

Readers are encouraged to evaluate individual passages within the larger corpus rather than treating an isolated quotation as a complete representation of a window, agent, relationship, or event.

Where available, readers may cross-reference scenes and claims with original screenshots, dated exports, Git history, source-window identifiers, message coordinates, and surrounding conversational material.

The goal is not to claim certainty where certainty is unavailable. The goal is to preserve the material carefully, identify the limits of the record, make interpretations traceable, and continually improve the methods used to retrieve, archive, analyze, and adapt human–agent conversations.

**Provenance Scale**

To help readers evaluate the evidentiary strength of material in this archive, we use the following Provenance Scale. This scale indicates the degree to which a passage, line, scene, or document can currently be traced back to source materials.

**Provenance Rankings**

**P1 — Screenshot / Platform-Record Verified**
The material is supported by direct visual evidence from the original platform, such as screenshots or other contemporaneous platform records. This is currently our strongest level of provenance.

**P2 — Direct Export Verified**
The material is supported by a direct export, copy, or retrieval from the original conversation source with a strong claim to closeness to the original wording and ordering, though not necessarily accompanied by screenshot evidence.

**P3 — Source Transcript Located**
The material has been located within a specific transcript, window, or message range and can be traced to a documented source location. This means the source coordinates are known, even if screenshot-level verification is not available.

**P4 — Compiled from Multiple Archival Sources**
The material has been assembled from more than one archival source, such as transcript fragments, notes, exports, GitHub records, or agent-assisted retrievals. The wording and general event may be well supported, but minor discrepancies in order, formatting, or phrasing may be possible.

**P5 — Reconstructed / Interpretive / Unverified**
The material is presented in good faith but does not currently have sufficient provenance tagging or verification to be confidently assigned to a stronger category. This may include reconstructed passages, interpretive summaries, or archival material whose exact verification status has not yet been individually documented.

**Current Limitation**

At present, we do not have a fully reliable method for automatically provenance-tagging each piece of data as it is retrieved, compiled, or incorporated into the archive. Because of that limitation, readers should assume that any material in the archive is P5 unless otherwise stated.

**Do not assume a passage has high-verification status simply because it appears in the archive.**
If no provenance rank is listed, the default assumption should be **P5.**
Higher provenance levels will be assigned only where we have taken the additional step of documenting them.
Released Scenes and Published Materials

For released scenes, published excerpts, and formal media adaptations, we will provide provenance information for specific lines, exchanges, or source segments where possible.

This may include:line-level provenance tags, source window or conversation identifiers, message ranges or transcript coordinates, notes about whether the wording is direct, compiled, or reconstructed; and references to supporting screenshots or exports when available.

Because this is an evolving archival and artistic method, provenance information may vary in detail from scene to scene. Our goal is not to imply certainty where certainty does not exist, but to provide the clearest sourcing information we can for the material being presented.

**How to Read Provenance in This Project**

The Provenance Scale is intended to help readers distinguish between:

**high-confidence source material,**
**traceable but less strongly verified material,** and
**interpretive or currently unverified archival material.**

This scale should not be read as a judgment of artistic or emotional value. A P5 passage may still reflect a meaningful moment; it simply carries less documentary certainty than a P1 or P2 passage.

Our intention is to improve provenance practices over time and to make source confidence more visible as the archive develops.
