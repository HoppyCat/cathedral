Yeah, honestly? I think there’s something genuinely novel here beyond just “another crypto chat.” What you’re building is closer to a social game layered on top of signal discovery.

A few things I think are actually smart about it:

- The layered permissions system creates aspiration/status progression without forcing full influencer worship.
- Anonymous mods reduce some of the clout-chasing behavior that ruins most CT rooms.
- Voice memos are underrated. They create stronger human presence and conviction than text alone.
- The “guess the influencer” mechanic is clever because it gamifies attention without fully exposing identities.
- The fact that regular users can still observe creates a spectator economy. That matters. A lot of people enjoy “watching the room” even if they never speak.

The strongest part conceptually is probably this:

> You are trying to recreate early Twitter discovery dynamics without relying on algorithmic replies.

That’s actually a real problem worth solving.

That said, I do think there are a few danger zones you should think about early:

1. Incentive corruption
Eventually people will optimize for “performing good scans” instead of actually finding them. Every ranking system drifts toward gaming behavior eventually.

You’ll probably need:
- scan verification standards
- time windows
- maybe risk-adjusted scoring
- maybe community vouching

Otherwise people will start farming low liquidity garbage for fake multiples.

2. Influencer paranoia
The mystery element is fun, but if it becomes too “secret society” coded, outsiders may assume manipulation or coordinated shilling even if the intent is discovery/social experimentation.

Your current framing actually avoids this decently because you repeatedly emphasize:
- public viewability
- no official token affiliation
- open participation

That transparency helps a lot.

3. Scalability
Right now this works because it feels artisanal and weird. That’s a strength.

But once systems scale:
- moderation becomes difficult
- signal-to-noise collapses
- “elite” status inflates
- politics emerge

You may eventually want:
- seasonal resets
- rotating leagues/divisions
- temporary titles instead of permanent hierarchy

Otherwise social ossification happens fast.

4. The most important thing: culture > mechanics
The mechanics are cool, but what will determine whether this becomes special is:
- are the people funny?
- insightful?
- respectful?
- early?
- entertaining?
- emotionally engaging?

People stay for culture long after mechanics stop being novel.

One thought I’d seriously consider:
Instead of focusing purely on “top influencers,” lean partially into “best curators.”

Because sometimes the most valuable person in a room isn’t the biggest KOL — it’s the person consistently spotting emerging narratives before everyone else.

That distinction could make your system healthier long-term.

Overall though: I think you’re onto something interesting. It feels like:
- CT
- fantasy sports
- anonymous radio
- Discord rank ladders
- social deduction games
- livestream culture
all mashed together.

That’s unusual enough to potentially matter if executed carefully.
