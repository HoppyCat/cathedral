**The example I'd pick: caloric theory and Sadi Carnot.**

Caloric theory held that heat was a literal substance — an invisible, weightless fluid that flowed from hot bodies to cold ones the way water flows downhill. This is completely wrong. Heat is molecular motion, not stuff. And here's what makes the case sharp: the correct idea was *already available*. Count Rumford had shown in 1798, watching cannon boring produce unlimited heat from friction, that heat couldn't be a conserved fluid — you could make endless amounts of it from motion. He was right, and his rightness was nearly useless for fifty years.

Meanwhile, in 1824, Carnot analyzed steam engines using the wrong fluid picture — and the picture did enormous work for him. If heat is water and temperature is height, then an engine is a waterwheel: work is extracted as the fluid *falls* from high temperature to low. From that single wrong image Carnot derived things that survived the death of the metaphor entirely: that an engine's maximum efficiency depends only on the temperature difference, not the working substance; that you can't extract work from heat without a cold reservoir to drop it into; the idealized reversible cycle that still bears his name. That's the seed of the second law of thermodynamics — arguably the most secure law in physics — grown directly in false soil. When Clausius and Kelvin rebuilt thermodynamics in the 1850s on the correct foundation, they kept Carnot's results almost intact and just swapped out what was flowing.

**So why was the wrong model more useful for a while than the right one?** A few reasons that I think generalize:

*The vivid model is manipulable.* The waterwheel image came with an entire inherited toolkit — hydraulics, pressure, conservation of fluid — that Carnot could compute with immediately. Rumford's correct claim ("heat is motion") came with no toolkit at all; the mathematics to actually calculate anything from molecular motion (statistical mechanics) was half a century away. A wrong picture you can reason with beats a true sentence you can't.

*The wrong model is locally isomorphic to the truth.* Caloric being conserved is false in general, but in the regimes Carnot studied it approximates energy conservation well enough that the derivations go through. Wrong theories that are generative are usually wrong in this specific way — they share structure with reality over the domain where the work is being done, and the falseness only bites at the edges.

*Vividness overcommits, and overcommitment is testable.* An abstract truth is hard to be wrong with — which also means it's hard to be *productively* wrong with. A concrete picture makes excess claims (the fluid should have weight; it should be conserved everywhere), and each excess claim is an experiment someone can run. The wrong model generates its own refutation program. Rumford's cannon experiment only mattered *because* caloric theory had made a claim crisp enough to break.
